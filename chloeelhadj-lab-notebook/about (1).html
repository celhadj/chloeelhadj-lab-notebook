About

I study Political Science and Art History. I build AI prototypes for tea companies and auction houses.

Those two sentences should feel like they don't belong together. That gap is the point. I'm not trying to become a software engineer — I'm trying to become the person who can sit inside a business that runs on expertise and taste, and tell you, specifically, which parts of it AI actually changes.

Where this started

At Harney & Sons, my job had nothing to do with AI. But I kept watching the same wholesale order get manually checked, three or four times, by people who clearly had better things to do. Nobody asked me to fix it. I mapped it anyway, because the workflow itself was the interesting part — more interesting than anything I'd have learned from a lecture on machine learning.

What I actually do

I don't train models. I sit in a workflow long enough to see what's actually slow versus what everyone assumes is slow, then prototype the smallest thing that tests whether AI helps — using tools like Claude and Structify, the way an operator would, not the way an engineer would. Then I show the thing that came out of it, not a slide describing it.

Where this is going

I want a career at the intersection of wealth management and fine art finance — both fields where the entire product is trust, and where almost no one is thinking carefully about where AI fits without cheapening that trust. This lab is me building that judgment before anyone is paying me to have it.
