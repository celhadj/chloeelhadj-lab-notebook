Harney & Sons — Wholesale Order Routing

I mapped how wholesale orders move through Harney & Sons, found the one manual check consuming the most operator time, and built a prototype that tests whether an AI-assisted check can absorb it — without removing the human sign-off that makes this a trust business in the first place.

The business problem

Every wholesale order at Harney & Sons — from a specialty grocer reordering its usual case count to a hotel placing a first-time bulk order — goes through the same manual verification step before it's released to fulfillment. That step exists for a good reason: pricing tiers, minimums, and account-specific terms are genuinely inconsistent across a wholesale book built up over decades. The problem is that the check doesn't distinguish between a routine reorder and an order that actually needs a person's judgment — both get the same manual treatment, which means the team's attention is spent evenly across orders that don't deserve it evenly. needs your input → who specifically felt this, and how often

The AI opportunity — and where it stops

The opportunity is narrow on purpose: a model that reads an incoming order against account history and flags only the ones that deviate from that account's normal pattern — new SKU, unusual volume, a price that doesn't match the standing agreement. Everything that matches the pattern clears without a person touching it.

What doesn't change: any order the model flags still goes to a person, and the person still has final say on anything involving a new account or a negotiated term. The judgment in this business lives in relationships built over years — that's not a step to automate, it's the step the automation exists to protect time for.
