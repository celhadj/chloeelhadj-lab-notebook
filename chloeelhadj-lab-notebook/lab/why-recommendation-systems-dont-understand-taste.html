Case Study 02 — Why Recommendation Systems Don't Understand Taste

Recommendation systems can predict what you'll listen to, but rarely ask why you actually love something. This project investigates whether a short conversation — asking a listener what draws them to a song, rather than assuming their taste from behavior or audio features alone — produces a recommendation the listener recognizes as accurate. The prototype below is the current test of that idea.

The hypothesis

People often know why they love a song. Most recommendation systems never ask — they infer taste from listening behavior or acoustic similarity instead. Large language models make it possible to have a short conversation before recommending anything at all. This prototype tests whether that conversation produces an understanding of a listener's taste that the listener actually recognizes as their own.

The prototype

A three-screen interaction: pick a song, answer a short adaptive interview (three to six questions, each one generated from what was just said rather than a fixed form), then confirm or edit an AI-written "understanding statement" before any recommendation is generated. Recommendations are produced only from the confirmed statement, each tied explicitly to the part of it they match.

The problem: existing recommendation systems begin with the song, not the listener — collaborative filtering infers taste from behavioral patterns across many users, content-based filtering infers it from measurable audio properties. Neither asks a listener to explain themselves, and neither can represent a reason a listener would recognize as their own.

What I built: a conversational interaction model. The system opens with one fixed question, then generates every subsequent question live from the conversation so far, deciding for itself when it has enough to proceed (between three and six questions). It then writes a short understanding statement in its own words, which the listener can edit freely before confirming — recommendations are generated only from whatever text is confirmed, never from the AI's first draft directly.

What changed along the way: the project originally became an increasingly detailed ontology of musical taste — ten to twelve dimensions, evidence-grounded concepts, confidence scores, an ontology-refinement tool. That work surfaced real methodological insight, but it was answering a different question than the one this project started with: not "can taste be represented richly?" but "does asking a listener help a system understand them better than assuming does?" The ontology was never actually tested against that question, because every version of it was single-shot — one description in, one interpretation out. This conversational redesign is the first version that actually tests it.

What I learned: the clearest signal this design produces is how much a listener has to edit the AI's understanding statement before confirming it. Heavy edits mean the conversation didn't work; light edits mean it did. That's a simpler, more honest measure of success than anything the ontology work produced, and it fell directly out of choosing this interaction pattern rather than out of any additional engineering.

What this project taught me

qualitative coding · conversational AI design · human-in-the-loop confirmation · recommendation system paradigms · research question discipline · knowing when to abandon work that answers the wrong question
