Why Recommendation Systems Don't Understand Taste

Recommendation systems can predict what you are likely to listen to, or identify patterns in what you enjoy listening to, but they rarely ask why you actually love something. This project investigates whether a short conversation, asking a listener what draws them to a song, rather than assuming their taste from behavior features alone, can produce recommendations that feel more personally meaningful and accurate. The prototype below is the current test of that idea.

The Hypothesis

People often know why they love a song, but lots of recommendation systems infer taste without asking listeners to explain those reasons. Instead, they define similarity through behavioral patterns and statistical relationships. Large language models can engage in a short conversation before recommending anything. This prototype tests whether that conversation produces an understanding of taste that the listener recognizes as their own.

The Prototype

A three-screen conversation: pick a song, answer a short adaptive sequence of questions (three to six questions, each generated from what was just said rather than a fixed form), then confirm or edit an AI-written "understanding statement" before any recommendation is generated. Recommendations are produced only from the confirmed statement, and each recommendation is tied explicitly to the reason the listener gave for liking the original song. The prototype does not try to find songs that are most similar to the original. Instead, it tries to find songs that satisfy the listener's own reason for loving the original, while remaining within a believable musical context.

The Problem: Existing recommendation systems begin with the song, not the listener. Many recommendations will use either collaborative filtering, which infers taste from behavioral patterns across many users, and content-based filtering, inferring taste from measurable audio properties. These appraoches are effective at prediction, but neither recommendation system asks a listener to explain themselves, and neither is designed to capture the subjective explanation or reason a listener would particularly like a song.

What I Built: A conversational interaction model. The system opens with one fixed question, then generates every subsequent question live from the conversation, determining when it has enough information to proceed (between three and six questions). It then writes a short understanding statement in its own words, which the listener can edit freely before confirming. Recommendations are generated only from whatever text is confirmed, never from the AI's first draft directly. The goal is not to extract every possible dimension of taste, but to reach a sufficient understanding with minimal user effort.

Project Evolution: The project originally became an increasingly detailed framework for representing musical taste. It had evidence-grounded concepts, an ontology-refinement tool, and confidence scores. This work surfaced real methodological insight, but it was answering a different question than the one this project started with: not "can taste be represented richly?" but "does asking a listener help a system understand them better than assuming does?" The ontology was never actually tested against that question, because every version of it was single-shot — one description in and one interpretation out. This conversational redesign is the first version that actually tests the original hypothesis

What I Learned: The clearest signal this design produces is how much a listener has to edit the AI's understanding statement before confirming it. The amount and type of editing became a signal of whether the system understood the listener's intended meaning. Small corrections suggest alignment, while fundamental rewrites reveal where the system misunderstood the listener. That's a simpler, more honest measure of success than anything the ontology work produced, and it fell directly out of choosing this interaction pattern rather than out of any additional engineering.
