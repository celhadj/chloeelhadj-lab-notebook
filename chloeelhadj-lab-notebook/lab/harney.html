Harney & Sons — Five Pipelines, One Pattern

During my internship at Harney & Sons, I built five data pipelines using Structify — each one turning a blind spot in the company's credit, inventory, or receivables operations into a live dashboard. Real account names and dollar figures stay internal. The architecture, the decisions, and what I learned building them are the real evidence, and none of that needs a real number to be true.

01 · Credit memo categorization

The problem: a large share of Harney's credit memos had no structured reason code attached — just free-text comments. That meant a large share of the money the company was crediting back to customers had no auditable explanation behind it.

What I built: a pipeline that pulled a rolling window of credit memos, kept structured reason codes as the source of truth where they existed, and built a confidence-scored inference engine that recovered a likely category from free-text notes for the undocumented ones — with a full keyword audit trail so nobody had to take the inference on faith.

What it found: credit exposure wasn't spread evenly across customers. A small number of accounts accounted for a disproportionate share of total credits issued — a real concentration-risk finding, not just a data-quality footnote.

What I learned: keeping "verified" and "inferred" as two separate dashboards, instead of blending them into one number, mattered more than the accuracy of the inference itself. A confident-looking blended total would have been more dangerous than an honest, lower-confidence estimate.

02 · Expired payment method outreach

This is the one with the cleanest before/after, so it's the one I diagrammed.

The problem: accounts with expired or incomplete card information sat unnoticed until an order actually failed. The instinct once someone did notice was "alert everyone flagged" — regardless of whether the account still ordered anything.

What I built: a pipeline that filtered flagged accounts down to active, repeat customers, drafted outreach tailored to who needed to see it (sales rep, customer, or accounts receivable), and posted every draft to an internal review channel for human approval before anything went out.

What I learned: the real design decision wasn't the extraction, it was the filter — a smaller, correctly-targeted list beat a longer, noisier one. I also learned mid-build that sales reps don't have direct system access, which changed who the emails actually needed to be addressed to.

03 · Inventory stock-out detection & forecasting

The problem: when a product line went out of stock, nobody could quickly tell whether it was a packaging shortage or an ingredient shortage — so every investigation started from zero.

What I built: a pipeline that classified every stock-out by root cause, forecasted risk across short-, medium-, and longer-term windows, and ranked what needed reordering by urgency.

What it found: stock-outs didn't just pause demand temporarily. Orders for the affected product nearly disappeared entirely during the gap, rather than customers waiting it out — which reframes a stock-out from an inventory inconvenience into a direct revenue event.

What I learned: monthly averages hid the real risk. A small number of very large, coordinated orders from a handful of major accounts created demand spikes many times the daily average — and that's what actually broke inventory planning, not gradual demand growth.

04 · Accounts receivable health dashboard

The problem: collections prioritization was manual and reactive. Nobody had a single ranked view of which overdue accounts actually mattered most.

What I built: a pipeline that aged every outstanding invoice, aggregated by customer, flagged the accounts driving a disproportionate share of total receivables, and produced one composite priority score per account blending size, age, and payment-history patterns.

What it found: a very small number of accounts represented a large majority of total outstanding receivables — a concentration risk worth surfacing to leadership on its own, independent of any single overdue invoice.

05 · Executive consolidation dashboard

The problem: leadership had no single live view spanning credit risk, inventory health, sales, and payment compliance — each lived in a different report, updated on a different schedule.

What I built: a real-time dashboard pulling live data across all four areas at once.

What I learned: partway through, one data facet turned out to be unreliable. Shipping four dependable views instead of five including one broken one was itself a real product decision, not just a technical fallback — a smaller, trustworthy dashboard beats a comprehensive, unreliable one.

The pattern across all five

None of these were hard because the data extraction was hard. Structify made pulling the data the easy part. Every one of these projects came down to the same three judgment calls: what to trust versus what to infer, what to filter out versus surface, and who specifically should see the result before anything became automatic. That's the actual skill this internship built — not a tool, a habit of asking those three questions before writing a single line of a pipeline.
