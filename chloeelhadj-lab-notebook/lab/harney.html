Case Study No. 1: AI for Business Operations

During a summer internship at a family-owned manufacturing company, I designed five AI-enabled pipelines to address operational challenges across accounting, inventory, and finance. This case study focuses on the reasoning behind each project: the business problem, the approach, and what I learned in the process. This case study and all of its examples have been anonymized to protect operational data and internal business processes. All examples reflect the original technological architecture and design decisions while omitting identifying information.

01 · Recovering Missing Credit Memo Categories

The problem: a large share of the organization's credit memos had no structured reason code attached — just free-text comments. That meant a large share of the money the organization was crediting back to the accounts had no auditable explanation behind it.

What I built: an AI prototype that pulled a rolling window of credit memos, kept structured reason codes as the source of truth, where they existed, and built a confidence-scored tab that recovered a likely category from free-text notes for the undocumented ones — with a full keyword audit trail so nobody had to take the assumption on faith.

Key insight: The analysis demonstrated that operational issues were highly concentrated rather than evenly distributed, reinforcing the value of prioritization over blanket reporting.

What I learned: keeping "verified" and "inferred" as two separate dashboards, instead of blending them into one number, mattered more than the accuracy of the estimation itself. A confident-looking blended total would have been more dangerous than an honest, lower-confidence estimate.

02 · Prioritizing Expired Payment Methods

This is the one with the cleanest before/after, so it's the one I diagrammed.

The problem: entities with expired or incomplete card information sat unnoticed until an order actually failed. The instinct once someone did notice was "alert everyone flagged" — regardless of whether the account still ordered anything.

What I built: an AI tool that filtered flagged accounts down to active, repeat accounts, drafted outreach tailored to who needed to see it (relationship manager, account, or finance team), and posted every draft to an internal review channel for human approval before anything went out.

What I learned: the real design decision wasn't the extraction, it was the filter — a smaller, correctly-targeted list beat a longer, noisier one. I also learned mid-build that relationship manager didn't have direct system access, which changed who the emails actually needed to be addressed to.

03 · Understanding Inventory Stockouts

The problem: when a product family went out of stock, nobody could quickly tell whether it was a packaging shortage or a component shortage — so every investigation started from zero.

What I built: a pipeline that classified every stock-out by root cause, forecasted risk across short-, medium-, and longer-term windows, and ranked what needed reordering by urgency.

Key insight: stock-outs didn't just pause demand temporarily. The analysis illustrated how stock-outs can suppress demand rather than simply delaying it, reinforcing the importance of proactive inventory planning.

What I learned: monthly averages hid the real risk. A small number of very large, coordinated orders from a handful of major customer records created demand spikes many times the daily average — and that's what actually broke inventory planning, not gradual demand growth.

04 · Finance Operations Dashboard

The problem: collections prioritization was manual and reactive. Nobody had a single ranked view of which overdue operational records actually mattered most.

What I built: an AI workflow that aged every outstanding invoice, aggregated by accounts, flagged the accounts driving a disproportionate share of total receivables, and produced one composite priority score per account blending size, age, and payment-history patterns.

Key insight: The dashboard highlighted how concentration analysis can reveal risks that are difficult to identify when reviewing individual invoices in isolation.

05 · Executive Operations Dashboard

The problem: leadership had no single live view spanning finance, operations, inventory, commercial performance— each lived in a different report, updated on a different schedule.

What I built: a dashboard pulling live data across all four areas at once.

What I learned: partway through, one data facet turned out to be unreliable. Shipping four dependable views instead of five including one broken one was itself a real product decision, not just a technical fallback — a smaller, trustworthy dashboard beats a comprehensive, unreliable one.

The pattern across all five

None of these were hard because the data extraction was hard. AI made pulling the data the easy part. Every one of these projects came down to the same three judgment calls: what to trust versus what to infer, what to filter out versus surface, and who specifically should see the result before anything became automatic. That became the real lesson of the project. Building pipelines was only part of the work. The harder challenge was deciding what to trust, infer, filter, and where human judgment should remain part of the process.
