Case Study 01 — AI Implementation in Consumer Goods

During a summer internship at a family-owned manufacturing company, I designed five AI-enabled pipelines to address operational challenges across accounting, inventory, and finance. This case study focuses on the reasoning behind each project: the business problem, the approach, and what I learned in the process. This case study and all of its examples have been anonymized to protect operational data and internal business processes. All examples reflect the original technological architecture and design decisions while omitting identifying information.

01 · Recovering Missing Credit Memo Categories

Across the company's delivery routes, credit memos had no single view connecting which clients, which drivers, and which reason codes were actually driving them. Along the way, a meaningful subset of memos turned out to have no reason code at all — recovered from free-text notes with a full confidence-scored audit trail.

Read full breakdown

The problem: credit memos issued across the company's delivery routes had no single view connecting which clients, which drivers, and which reason codes were actually driving them — so patterns were invisible route by route.

What I built: a pipeline matching credit memos to invoices by customer and item (leaning on the platform's built-in document references where available), scoped to the last quarter as a first pass toward eventually covering all historical data. It surfaced which clients, drivers, and reason codes — damaged item, picking error, customer/courier return — were most associated with credit memos across each route.

Key discovery: while reviewing the reason-code data, I found a meaningful subset of credit memos had no reason code recorded at all — just free-text notes. That became a second layer of the project: a confidence-scored tool that recovered a likely category from the notes, kept in a separate "inferred" view from verified codes so nothing looked more certain than it was, with a full keyword audit trail behind every inferred category.

What I learned: keeping "verified" and "inferred" as two separate views, instead of blending them into one number, mattered more than the accuracy of the estimation itself. A confident-looking blended total would have been more dangerous than an honest, lower-confidence estimate.

02 · Prioritizing Expired Payment Methods

Expired cards were only ever caught reactively — after a payment already failed. This pipeline flips that: it proactively flags cards approaching expiration, drafts outreach before the payment is due, and logs every alert and customer response back to their profile.

The problem: expired cards were only caught through manual reconciliation — by the time anyone noticed, a payment attempt had already failed, and staff had to reach out, wait for a response, and update the card before it could be reprocessed.

What I built: an AI pipeline that proactively flagged accounts with cards approaching expiration — not just already-expired ones — filtered to active, repeat accounts, drafted outreach tailored to who needed to see it (relationship manager, account, or finance team) ahead of the payment due date, and logged every alert and the customer's response back to their profile. Every draft still went through a private review channel for human approval before anything went out.

What I learned: the real design decision wasn't the extraction, it was the filter — a smaller, correctly-targeted list beat a longer, noisier one. I also learned mid-build that relationship manager didn't have direct system access, which changed who the alerts actually needed to be addressed to.

03 · Understanding Inventory Stockouts

When a product family went out of stock, nobody could quickly tell whether it was a packaging or component shortage. An anomaly detector built to catch real sales gaps in tin finished-goods data uncovered something else first: its own false positives, caused by a truncated data pull.

The problem: nobody had a systematic way to tell whether an unusual dip in sales for a finished-goods tin product was a real stock-out or just noise in the data.

Phase 1 — initial detection: I built an anomaly detector scanning 30 days of invoice data for tin products, watching for sales gaps (7+ consecutive days with no invoices), customer concentration (over 80% of revenue from one buyer), and cadence drops (week-over-week quantity drops over 60%). It flagged 64 anomalies — 62 concentration flags and 2 sales gaps.

Phase 2 — narrowing to what mattered: most of the concentration flags turned out to be expected — private-label items are inherently single-customer. I focused on the two sales-gap findings as the more actionable signal and built a 60-day daily timeline to visualize them.

Phase 3 — the discovery that mattered: to confirm these were real stock-outs, I pulled inventory levels for the gap periods. Direct queries against the source platform showed invoices were actually flowing continuously the whole time — both flagged gaps were false positives, caused by a bulk 30-day data pull that had silently truncated and missed records for those specific items.

Phase 4 — rebuilding with verification: I rebuilt the pipeline to properly paginate the full dataset (500K+ line items), detect gaps from the corrected data, then verify every flagged gap directly against the source platform's invoices and delivery notes before calling it real, enriching confirmed gaps with current warehouse stock and shipping activity.

The replenishment side: alongside the anomaly work, I added one master summary tab — not a rebuild of the existing workbook — pulling stock levels, burn rate (from production order history and purchase receipt patterns), and what's already on order from vendors into a single filterable view, sorted by urgency and filterable by item group (tins, labels, boxes). Items are flagged CRITICAL at two months or less of supply remaining, WARNING at four months or less, and OK otherwise — so a planner walking into a purchasing meeting has the full position without clicking through multiple sheets.

What I learned: an anomaly detector is only as trustworthy as the data feeding it. The two flags that looked most "real" on the first pass turned out to be an artifact of truncated data, not an actual business signal — building in direct verification against source data, rather than trusting the first pass, was the actual lesson of this project. On the replenishment side, the fix didn't need to be a rebuild: adding one clear summary view mattered more than restructuring anything that already worked.

04 · Finance Operations Dashboard

The data lived in an internal accounting platform, but in a form that only supported sending payment-reminder emails — no patterned or cross-referenced view of who was actually driving the tail. This pipeline built that view: aging buckets, which accounts drive the tail, and which accounts trend toward withholding payment over time.

The problem: AR data was readily available in the company's internal accounting platform, but structured in a way that only supported sending payment reminders — no patterned or cross-referenced analysis of who was actually driving overdue balances, or who kept doing it.

What I built: a live AR health check that aged every outstanding invoice into buckets (current, 30, 60, 90, 120+ days), aggregated by account, flagged which accounts were driving a disproportionate share of the tail, and separately flagged accounts that trend toward withholding payment over time — not just accounts that happen to be overdue right now. A composite priority score blends account size, invoice age, and payment-history pattern into one ranked view.

Key insight: concentration analysis surfaced risk that was invisible when reviewing individual invoices in isolation — a handful of accounts drove a disproportionate share of total exposure, and some of them were repeat offenders rather than one-off late payments.

05 · Executive Operations Dashboard

Leadership had no single bird's-eye view spanning AR, deliveries, and inventory — each lived in a different report, updated on a different schedule. This dashboard pulled live data across all of them at once.

The problem: leadership had no single live view spanning AR, deliveries, and inventory — each lived in a different report, updated on a different schedule.

What I built: the scope grew during the build to four live facets — AR credits, inventory, sales, and payments — pulling directly from source on every run.

What I learned: partway through, one data facet turned out to be unreliable. Shipping four dependable views instead of five including one broken one was itself a real product decision, not just a technical fallback — a smaller, trustworthy dashboard beats a comprehensive, unreliable one.

The pattern across all five

None of these were hard because the data extraction was hard. AI made pulling the data the easy part. Every one of these projects came down to the same three judgment calls: what to trust versus what to infer, what to filter out versus surface, and who specifically should see the result before anything became automatic. That became the real lesson of the project. Building pipelines was only part of the work. The harder challenge was deciding what to trust, infer, filter, and where human judgment should remain part of the process.
