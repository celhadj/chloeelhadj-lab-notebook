AI Implementation in Consumer Goods

During my summer internship, I was tasked with identifying operational bottlenecks across inventory, accounting, and production. I explored whether AI could simplify repetitive work while leaving important decisions in human hands. This page documents five small implementation projects. Each focuses less on the technology itself and more on the reasoning behind it: the problem, the design decisions, and what I learned. Please note: company names, data, and identifying details have been intentionally anonymized.

Project 1 · Recovering Missing Credit Memo Categories

Across the company's delivery routes, credit memos had no single view that connected which clients, drivers, and reason codes were actually driving them. After the first data extraction, it was revealed that several memos turned out to have no reason code at all. For the credit memos with no reason code, I pulled data from free-text notes with a full confidence-scored audit trail.

Read full breakdown

Identified Problem: Credit memos issued across the company's delivery routes had no single view connecting which clients, which drivers, and which reason codes were actually causing them, so patterns were invisible route by route.

What I built: An internal tool matching credit memos to invoices by customer, delivery route, and items that was intentionally limited to the last quarter (a first pass toward eventually covering all historical data). This project revealed (1) trends by month, (2) which customers are affected most by each reason (e.g., damaged goods, picking error, customer returns), (3) vendors causing the most errors, (4) risk scores by reason, and (5) percent and count of credits per reason.

Key Discovery: While reviewing the reason-code data, I found a meaningful subset of credit memos had no reason code recorded at all, just free-text notes. This became a second layer of the project: a confidence-scored tool that created a "likely" category from the notes. This category was created in a separate "inferred" window from verified codes so nothing looked more certain than it was, with a full keyword audit trail behind every inferred category.

What I learned: Keeping "verified" and "inferred" as two separate views, instead of blending them into one number, mattered more than the accuracy of the estimation itself. A confident-looking, mixed total would have been more dangerous than a clear, lower-confidence estimate.

Project 2 · Prioritizing Expired Payment Methods

Expired cards were only ever caught reactively, after a payment had already failed. This system flips that: it proactively flags cards approaching expiration, drafts outreach immediately, and logs every alert and customer response back to their profile.

View dashboard
Read full breakdown

The Problem: Expired cards were only caught through manual reconciliation. A payment attempt had already failed, staff had to reach out, wait for a esponse, and update the card before the invoice could be reprocessed.

What I Built: A dashboard that proactively flagged accounts with cards approaching expiration, not just already-expired ones. These findings were filtered to active accounts and repeat accounts. The AI tool then drafted outreach tailored to who needed to see it (relationship manager, account, or finance team) ahead of the payment due date, and logged every alert and the customer's response back to their profile. Every draft went through a private review channel for human approval before anything went out.

What I Learned: The real design decision wasn't the extraction, it was the filter. Having a smaller, correctly-targeted list beat a longer, noisier one. I also learned mid-build that relationship managers didn't have direct system access, which changed who the alerts actually needed to be addressed to.

Project 3 · Finance Operations Dashboard

Data of outstanding invoice payments lived in an internal accounting platform, but in a form that only supported sending payment-reminder emails; there was no patterned or cross-referenced view of who was actually driving the tail. This pipeline created a consolidated dashboard that allowed for the quick identification of aging buckets, which accounts drive the tail, and which accounts trend toward withholding payment over time.

View dashboard
Read full breakdown

The Problem: AR data was readily available in the company's internal accounting platform, but structured in a way that primarily supported sending payment reminders, no patterned or cross-referenced analysis of who was actually driving overdue balances, or who kept doing it.

What I Built: A live AR health check that aged every outstanding invoice into buckets (live, 30, 60, 90, 120+ days) aggregated by account. It also flagged which accounts were driving a disproportionate share of the tail, and which accounts trended toward withholding payment over time. Moreover, a multi-factor priority score combined the AR contribution %, days overdue, and payment behavior patterns for data-driven collection decisions.

Key Insight: A concentrated analysis surfaced a risk that has been invisible when reviewing individual invoices in isolation: a handful of accounts drove a disproportionate share of total exposure, and some of them were repeat offenders rather than one-off late payments.

Project 4 · Executive Operations Dashboard

Leadership had no single bird's-eye view spanning AR, deliveries, and inventory. Each lived in a different report and were updated on a different schedule. This dashboard pulled live data across all of them at once.

The Problem: Leadership had no single live view spanning AR, deliveries, and inventory. Each lived in a different report, updated on a different schedule.

What I Built: Data from four live aspects of the company, AR credits, inventory, sales, and payments are now pulled and organized within a prototype on every run of this dashboard.

What I Learned: Partway through this project, one category of extracted data turned out to be unreliable. Shipping four dependable views instead of five, which would have included one the broken one, was itself a real product decision, not just a technical fallback. Once again, I was taught that a smaller, trustworthy dashboard beats a comprehensive, unreliable one.

The pattern across all four

None of these projects were ultimately constrained by data extraction. Modern AI tools have made that relatively straightforward. The harder questions were deciding what to trust, what to infer, what to filter out, and where human judgment should remain part of the process. This was ultimately the lesson of my internship. Designing the worflows was often easier than deciding how they could be used.
