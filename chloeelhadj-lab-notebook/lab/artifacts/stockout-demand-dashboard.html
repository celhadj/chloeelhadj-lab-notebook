Key Insight

Inventory planning assumed demand would arrive gradually across the month. Instead, a small number of wholesale customers concentrated weeks of demand into a single purchasing event. The resulting spike depleted inventory before replenishment cycles could react.

The large-order problem: a single day this period saw an unusually large spike in order volume, driven by a handful of large wholesale accounts placing coordinated pre-buys — representing a large share of a typical month's volume compressed into one day.

Lessons Learned

  • Averaging hides the real risk. Monthly totals look stable, but daily granularity reveals swings many times larger than average — the planning window was too coarse.
  • Customer concentration is itself a risk factor. A small number of large accounts drive a large share of volume; when they pre-buy together, they create artificial peaks that break inventory systems.
  • Stock-outs change customer behavior, not just timing. Near-total suppression during gaps shows customers don't wait patiently — they go elsewhere or simply don't order.
  • Limited access blocks full root-cause analysis. Certain production and inventory tables weren't accessible, so the pipeline can see the demand side clearly but can only infer supply-side bottlenecks.
  • This scale breaks a spreadsheet approach. Millions of records across a large customer base can't be reviewed manually — automation surfaced a spike that would otherwise be buried.
