Key Decisions & Learnings

  • Bill-of-materials data wasn't accessible. A name-matching strategy links finished goods to their packaging and ingredients by product variety instead, working for the large majority of cases.
  • Seasonal detection is data-driven, not guesswork. A concentration test — checking whether a few months dominate total volume — separates seasonal items from steady sellers automatically.
  • Receipts stand in for true consumption data, since sales-side detail wasn't fully available; conservative estimates avoid over-ordering.
  • Purchase order status alone isn't reliable. Cross-referencing order lines against actual receipts correctly identifies over-delivered vs. short-shipped items.
  • A "committed inventory" flag proved unreliable, showing phantom commitments with no backing orders — on-hand quantity alone gives a simpler, more accurate reorder signal.
Why this matters: the shift is from reactive fire-fighting ("why did we run out?") to predictive planning ("when will we run out, and what specifically do we reorder today?") — with root-cause clarity that distinguishes packaging bottlenecks from ingredient bottlenecks, and vendor transparency around late or short deliveries.
